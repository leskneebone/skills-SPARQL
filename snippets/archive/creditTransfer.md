# 2. The explicit decision rule

### ‚ÄúCert IV is sufficient *if and only if*‚Ä¶‚Äù

A **Certificate IV in IT is sufficient** *if and only if* **all** of the following are true:

1. Francis‚Äôs role remains **primarily operational**, focused on:

   * system administration
   * configuration
   * implementation of predefined controls

   * following established procedures
   * applying controls specified by others

3. Francis is **not expected to**:

   * define or critique security requirements
   * contribute materially to risk assessments

A **Bachelor of IT becomes justified** if Francis is expected to:

* participate in **requirements definition**
* engage in **risk-based security discussions**
* influence decisions during system design or procurement
* act as a bridge between technical staff, vendors, and business stakeholders
* grow into a senior or advisory role over the life of the transformation program

## 2. Role of `skos:prefLabel`

**Purpose:** canonical name of the skill

In this use case:

* The prefLabel is what:

  * curriculum designers recognise
  * HR systems index
  * humans scan in tables and reports

Example:

```
skos:prefLabel "Assess cybersecurity risk"
```

Without a stable prefLabel:

* skills cannot be reliably compared
* reporting becomes noisy
* credit arguments become opaque

**PrefLabel is non-negotiable.**

---

## 3. Role of `skos:altLabel`

**Purpose:** vocabulary bridging

This is *crucial* for credit transfer.

Why?

* VET says ‚Äúrisk management‚Äù
* HE says ‚Äúrisk assessment‚Äù
* Workplace says ‚Äúsecurity risk‚Äù
* Vendors say ‚Äúthreat modelling‚Äù

AltLabels let you say:

> *These are linguistically different expressions of the same underlying skill.*

Example:

```
skos:altLabel "Cybersecurity risk management"
skos:altLabel "Security risk assessment"
skos:altLabel "Threat risk analysis"
```

Without altLabels:

* alignment requires manual interpretation
* automated or semi-automated matching fails
* cross-sector comparison becomes brittle

**AltLabel is what makes cross-system mapping plausible.**

---

## 4. Role of `skos:definition`

**Purpose:** boundary control and justification

Definitions answer:

* *What does this skill include?*
* *What does it explicitly not include?*

This matters because:

* Cert IV and Bachelor may both ‚Äúcover‚Äù something called ‚Äúrisk‚Äù
* But at **very different conceptual depths**

The definition is where you encode:

* scope
* intent
* expected level of reasoning

Example:

```
skos:definition "The ability to identify cybersecurity threats and vulnerabilities, assess likelihood and impact, and use this analysis to prioritise security controls and inform decision-making."
```

This is what lets you say:

> ‚ÄúThis Bachelor learning outcome genuinely maps to this skill, but this Cert IV unit only partially does.‚Äù

**Definitions are what make credit decisions defensible.**

---

## 5. Role of `schema:keywords` (yes, you are right)

This exercise has already relied on them implicitly.

**Keywords support:**

* discovery
* clustering
* loose matching
* evidence aggregation

They are especially useful where:

* curricula use long prose descriptions
* skills are embedded implicitly
* exact labels don‚Äôt line up

Example:

```

```

Keywords let you:

* surface candidate skills during mapping
* justify why a unit or learning outcome was considered relevant
* support explainable matching (‚Äúwe matched these because‚Ä¶‚Äù)

**Keywords are doing early-stage alignment work**, not final assertions.
